The supplied paper frames GDP as a useful but incomplete measure: national income helps explain production capacity, while population and freedom metrics add context about scale, opportunity, markets, and governance. Why GDP per person matters

A high GDP total can reflect either very high productivity or a very large population. The paper notes that GDP per capita normalizes output by population, making it a clearer comparison of average economic production across countries.

Key takeaways

Compact context from the paper and related country articles.

High-income comparison anchors

Ireland, Finland, and the United States anchor the higher-score group: Ireland pairs the strongest GDP per capita in this set with the top freedom score, Finland leads on property rights, and the United States supplies the largest GDP total.

Large-population development context

Bangladesh, China, Nigeria, Philippines, and Viet Nam show why population scale matters. Their GDP totals and consumer markets are large, but GDP per person and index components vary widely, so the paper's comparison reads output alongside institutional indicators.

Middle-income regional contrasts

El Salvador, Mexico, Thailand, Trinidad and Tobago, and Ukraine cluster around midrange freedom scores, but their GDP per capita, trade freedom, and financial freedom values differ enough to make side-by-side bar comparisons useful.

Lower-score institutional cases

Cameroon and Cuba broaden the comparison by showing lower overall scores and weaker component readings. Nigeria, Bangladesh, China, and Ukraine also illustrate how property rights and financial freedom can lag behind trade or business-freedom measures.

Source-linked country stories

AP News China price context, BBC Nigeria property-seizure reporting, and the CIA Bangladesh economy entry keep the dataset connected to real examples of consumer demand, governance, and development conditions.

All 15 countries retained
