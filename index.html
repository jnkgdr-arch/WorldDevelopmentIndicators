Reading GDP alongside institutions

The supplied paper frames GDP as a useful but incomplete measure: national income helps explain production capacity, while population and freedom metrics add context about scale, opportunity, markets, and governance. The charts below pair World Development Indicators style macroeconomic comparisons with 2025 Index of Economic Freedom categories to make those relationships easier to inspect.

Geographic heat map

GDP per capita shows output relative to population.

A high GDP total can reflect either very high productivity or a very large population. The paper notes that GDP per capita normalizes output by population, making it a clearer comparison of average economic production across countries.

Caption: GDP-per-person interpretation follows the BEA GDP primer, OpenStax GDP comparison discussion, and World Bank World Development Indicators source framing.

Geographic bubble chart

GDP and population reveal different kinds of economic weight.

Bubble size compares GDP while color compares population, showing why populous countries can be central to global demand even when per-person output differs. This context connects to the AP News discussion of consumer prices in China and the CIA World Factbook summary of Bangladesh's economy.

Caption: The visualization connects GDP, population, and consumer-demand context from AP News, the CIA World Factbook Bangladesh economy entry, and World Bank WDI.

Economic freedom geo chart

Overall economic freedom scores add an institutional lens.

The paper treats economic freedom as a complement to GDP comparisons: property rights, regulatory efficiency, open trade, and financial access can influence how people and firms participate in growth. The BBC article on Nigeria EFCC property seizures illustrates why governance and property-rights questions matter.

Caption: Overall scores and component definitions are based on the Heritage Foundation Index of Economic Freedom, with governance context from the BBC Nigeria EFCC article.
